Why this system exists

Lebanon's agricultural sector generates approximately USD 1.8 billion annually, roughly 3–5% of national GDP (IDAL, FAO). Of that, the Bekaa Valley produces around 40% of the country's cultivated output — including roughly 65% of all cereals (wheat, barley) and 65–70% of potato production. That means the Bekaa alone contributes on the order of USD 700 million per year to Lebanese agriculture, supporting tens of thousands of farm jobs and much of the country's food security.

Climate shifts are steadily eroding the narrow balance between winter rainfall and summer heat this region depends on. When the De Martonne Aridity Index falls below 20, rain-fed wheat stops being economically viable. Progressive desertification of the Bekaa could therefore cost Lebanon hundreds of millions of dollars per year in lost agricultural output and push an already crisis-stricken economy into deeper import dependency. BekaaSense turns eleven years of station-level temperature and precipitation data into forward-looking aridity forecasts at four LARI stations — Ammik, Doures, Ras Baalbeck and Tal Amara — so the Ministry of Agriculture, LARI, FAO Lebanon and farm cooperatives can answer one concrete question: is rain-fed wheat still viable here, and for how long?

Feature attribution (SHAP)

SHAP values decompose a single prediction into the contribution of each input feature. Green bars push the predicted index up (toward wetter conditions); red bars push it down (drier). The values are additive: the sum of the bars plus the model's baseline equals the final prediction. This is how we surface model reasoning instead of treating it as a black box.

Scope, limitations, and responsible use

Temporal scope

The system is trained on roughly eleven years of monthly station data (2015–present). Reliable predictive validity has been assessed only for horizons up to 24 months. The widening prediction intervals reflect this: short horizons (1–6 months) are operationally useful for seasonal planning, while horizons beyond two years are presented for illustrative purposes and should not inform binding decisions.

Spatial scope

Predictions are valid only for the four monitored stations (Ammik, Doures, Ras Baalbeck, Tal Amara). The system makes no claims about unmonitored locations; geographical interpolation would require a much denser sensor network than currently exists in the Bekaa.

Statistical significance of trend detection

Mann–Kendall trend tests across all four stations return p > 0.05. The apparent trends (Ammik wetting, the other three drying) are directionally consistent with regional climatology but cannot be claimed as statistically significant from this dataset alone. Formal trend detection would require 20–30 years of continuous observations.

Index formulation

The De Martonne aridity index is a first-order agroclimatic indicator combining precipitation and temperature. It does not incorporate wind speed, solar radiation, or actual evapotranspiration. A future iteration of BekaaSense is planned around SPEI once a longer homogeneous temperature record becomes available.

Distribution shift under climate change

Climate change is itself a distribution shift: the climate of 2015–2025 may not represent the climate of 2030 +. The system mitigates this by widening intervals with horizon, stratifying residuals by year so drift is visible, and disclosing this caveat here. It does not eliminate the risk.

Intended use

BekaaSense is a decision-support tool. It complements expert agronomic and hydrological judgement rather than replacing it. Outputs should be interpreted alongside on-the-ground knowledge of soil, irrigation infrastructure, market dynamics, and policy context.
