Why this system exists

Lebanon's agricultural sector generates approximately USD 1.8 billion annually, roughly 3–5% of national GDP (IDAL, FAO). Of that, the Bekaa Valley produces around 40% of the country's cultivated output — including roughly 65% of all cereals (wheat, barley) and 65–70% of potato production. That means the Bekaa alone contributes on the order of USD 700 million per year to Lebanese agriculture, supporting tens of thousands of farm jobs and much of the country's food security.

Climate shifts are steadily eroding the narrow balance between winter rainfall and summer heat this region depends on. When the De Martonne Aridity Index falls below 20, rain-fed wheat stops being economically viable. Progressive desertification of the Bekaa could therefore cost Lebanon hundreds of millions of dollars per year in lost agricultural output and push an already crisis-stricken economy into deeper import dependency. BekaaSense turns eleven years of station-level temperature and precipitation data into forward-looking aridity forecasts at four LARI stations — Ammik, Doures, Ras Baalbeck and Tal Amara — so the Ministry of Agriculture, LARI, FAO Lebanon and farm cooperatives can answer one concrete question: is rain-fed wheat still viable here, and for how long?

What does my aridity zone mean? — Decision guide

The De Martonne Aridity Index (DM = 12 × monthly rain ÷ (temperature + 10)) classifies each month into one of five zones. Use this table to translate the crop viability signal above into a concrete agricultural action. When the signal turns orange or red, consult the corresponding row below immediately.

🌊 Humid
DM > 30
Excellent water availability. All rain-fed crops are climatically viable. Soil moisture is typically adequate through the full growing season.
Maintain normal operations. Monitor for waterlogging or disease pressure in very wet months.
🌿 Sub-humid
DM 20–30
Good conditions with occasional water stress. Rain-fed wheat remains viable. Isolated dry spells possible in shoulder months.
Monitor weekly rainfall totals. Consider supplemental irrigation in individual dry months. No major concern for cereal viability.
⚠️ Semi-arid
DM 10–20
Water stress is likely. Rain-fed wheat is under pressure — yield losses of 20–50% have been documented at this index level in the Bekaa Valley.
Irrigation planning is essential. Switch to drought-tolerant varieties. Consider soil moisture sensors. Alert farm cooperatives early.
🔥 Arid
DM 5–10
Severe water deficit. Rain-fed agriculture is high-risk. Most traditional wheat cultivation is economically unviable at this index level.
Restrict rain-fed wheat. Prioritise water-efficient crops or fallow the land. Alert the Ministry of Agriculture and FAO Lebanon.
💥 Hyper-arid
DM < 5
Extreme drought. Agriculture is critically dependent on irrigation. Sustained months at this level indicate active desertification risk.
Activate emergency irrigation protocols. Consider crop fallowing. Engage national food security response. Document for climate adaptation records.

How each model works

BekaaSense runs six models on every forecast: three classical non-AI baselines and three machine-learning models. All six are evaluated on the same held-out test set so comparisons are completely fair. The baselines are included deliberately — they show the exact performance floor that the AI models must clear to justify their complexity.

Non-AI baselines — statistical & rule-based methods
📏
Linear Trend
Baseline — Regression

Draws one straight line per station through annual De Martonne averages over time. Assumes the climate changes at a constant rate every year. Fast and fully interpretable — but it cannot capture seasonal cycles or nonlinear climate responses. Think of it as drawing a ruler through a wavy line.

R² = −0.047 — worse than predicting the mean
🌊
SARIMA
Baseline — Regression

A classical time-series model that learns Lebanon's 12-month precipitation cycle directly from past data. It captures the summer–winter rhythm and recent trends, but cannot learn from external variables like temperature anomalies or multi-year drought indices. The strongest non-AI competitor.

R² = 0.837 • RMSE = 8.15 DM units
⚖️
Rule Baseline
Baseline — Classification

A single expert rule: "If recent rainfall is below the station average AND temperature is above normal → predict Drier-than-Normal." No data learning at all — pure climate domain knowledge encoded as a decision rule. Sets the floor that any algorithm must beat.

F1 = 0.816 with a single rule
AI-powered models — machine learning
🌿
Random Forest
AI — Best Forecaster

Trains 400 decision trees simultaneously, each on a different random slice of the data and features. The final prediction is the ensemble average — this smooths individual errors and captures nonlinear interactions between precipitation, temperature lags, and drought indices that SARIMA cannot express. Prediction intervals use split conformal prediction for a guaranteed 90%+ coverage.

R² = 0.940 • RMSE = 4.95 • Coverage = 93.8%
⚡
XGBoost Regressor
AI — Forecaster

Builds trees sequentially — each new tree corrects the errors of the previous ones (gradient boosting). Uses early stopping on the validation year (2022) to prevent overfitting. Reaches similar accuracy to Random Forest via a completely different algorithm, which is strong evidence that both results are genuine and reproducible.

R² = 0.928 • RMSE = 5.42 • Coverage = 94.4%
🎯
XGBoost Classifier
AI — Zone Classifier

A separate model that directly predicts the aridity zone from 24 current weather features — including the De Martonne index, seasonal encoding, and station identity. Trained independently from the regressors. Uses SMOTE-Tomek oversampling to handle the class imbalance between rare Humid months and dominant Hyper-arid months.

F1 = 0.979 • 140 / 144 test months correct

Scientific conclusions

All conclusions below are derived from the held-out test set only (144 station-months, 2023–present). No test data was used during training or hyperparameter selection.

1
Linear extrapolation cannot capture monthly aridity (R² = −0.047). The negative R² means the LinearTrend model is worse than simply predicting the historical average for every month. Monthly aridity in the Bekaa is driven by strong seasonal cycles and inter-annual variability, not a monotone trend. Naive climatological extrapolation is not a viable forecasting tool here.
2
Seasonality alone explains 83.7% of month-to-month variability (SARIMA R² = 0.837). The Mediterranean wet-winter / dry-summer cycle is so dominant that a model knowing only past values and the 12-month period achieves solid performance. This is the true baseline floor: any ML model must reproduce this seasonal pattern first, then contribute additional predictive power on top of it.
3
Machine learning reduces forecast error by 39% over the best statistical baseline (RF RMSE = 4.95 vs SARIMA RMSE = 8.15). The gain comes from learning nonlinear interactions among precipitation lags, rolling drought indices (SPI-3, SPI-6, SPI-12), and temperature anomalies that SARIMA cannot express mathematically. In practice, this means a ±5 DM uncertainty range instead of ±8 DM — enough to reliably straddle the DM = 20 wheat viability threshold.
4
Two independent ML algorithms converge to the same accuracy ceiling (RF R² = 0.940, XGB R² = 0.928). Random Forest (parallel averaging) and XGBoost (sequential boosting) use fundamentally different learning mechanisms yet achieve nearly identical performance. This convergence is strong evidence that R² ≈ 0.93–0.94 represents the true predictive ceiling of the 11-year dataset, not an artifact of one algorithm.
5
Aridity zone classification is near-perfect (F1 = 0.979, 140 / 144 correct). The classifier correctly identifies 97.9% of station-months in the test set. The 4 misclassifications all fall within 1 De Martonne unit of a zone boundary — genuinely ambiguous cases where even a domain expert using raw data would face the same difficulty.
6
Prediction intervals are reliable and conservative (RF actual coverage: 93.8%, XGB: 94.4% on a nominal 90% target). Using split conformal prediction with a finite-sample correction guarantees that the shaded forecast bands contain the true value at least 90% of the time on new data. The slight over-coverage (93–94% rather than 90%) means the system errs toward wider intervals — the correct behaviour for agricultural risk management, where false confidence is more dangerous than appropriate caution.

SHAP values decompose a single prediction into the contribution of each input feature. Green bars push the predicted index up (toward wetter conditions); red bars push it down (drier). Scope, limitations, and responsible use

Temporal scope

The system is trained on roughly eleven years of monthly station data (2015–present). Reliable predictive validity has been assessed only for horizons up to 24 months. The widening prediction intervals reflect this: short horizons (1–6 months) are operationally useful for seasonal planning, while horizons beyond two years are presented for illustrative purposes and should not inform binding decisions.

Spatial scope

Predictions are valid only for the four monitored stations (Ammik, Doures, Ras Baalbeck, Tal Amara). The system makes no claims about unmonitored locations; geographical interpolation would require a much denser sensor network than currently exists in the Bekaa.

Statistical significance of trend detection

Mann–Kendall trend tests across all four stations return p > 0.05. The apparent trends (Ammik wetting, the other three drying) are directionally consistent with regional climatology but cannot be claimed as statistically significant from this dataset alone. Formal trend detection would require 20–30 years of continuous observations.

Index formulation

The De Martonne aridity index is a first-order agroclimatic indicator combining precipitation and temperature. It does not incorporate wind speed, solar radiation, or actual evapotranspiration. A future iteration of BekaaSense is planned around SPEI once a longer homogeneous temperature record becomes available.

Distribution shift under climate change

Climate change is itself a distribution shift: the climate of 2015–2025 may not represent the climate of 2030 +. The system mitigates this by widening intervals with horizon, stratifying residuals by year so drift is visible, and disclosing this caveat here. It does not eliminate the risk.

Intended use

BekaaSense is a decision-support tool. It complements expert agronomic and hydrological judgement rather than replacing it. Outputs should be interpreted alongside on-the-ground knowledge of soil, irrigation infrastructure, market dynamics, and policy context.
